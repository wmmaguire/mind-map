## Value Proposition

Mindmap turns your writing, transcripts, and recorded conversations into an interactive concept graph — and then helps you read it back to yourself, surfacing the shape of your own thinking and the directions it is quietly leaning toward.

---

### Who it's for

Readers, writers, researchers, and reflective thinkers who want to see how their own material holds together — and where it might go next.

### What it does

- **Ingest** text, markdown, and recorded audio (transcribed automatically, with optional segment timestamps).
- **Analyze** each source with an LLM into a concept graph, and **merge** multiple sources in a single session.
- **Explore** the graph on an interactive canvas: pan, zoom, search, focus, minimap, and time-travel **playback** of how the graph grew.
- **Edit, generate, and explode** subgraphs from any node to develop an idea further.
- **Save and share** graphs (read-only share links) for later or for collaborators.
- **Insights** surface global network metrics and *Notable by centrality* (degree, betweenness, closeness, eigenvector).
- **Assess** produces a two-phase reading — claims about the map grounded in its named concepts, with clearly marked speculative extension — through interpretive or network-oriented guiding questions, in voice presets (Freud, Palahniuk, Murakami, Thompson, or custom) and adjustable length.

### Why it matters

Unstructured, free-flowing conversation has an explosive, flexible property: it expands in many directions at once while binding those directions in memorable ways. The network of thoughts takes on a life of its own — self-organizing, self-emergent. Reflecting on the loose associations that push and pull those concepts can uncover an underlying, subconscious pattern of thought: the rare finding behind déjà vu, paradoxical truths, and moments of ephemeral clarity.

You trail off on a tangent mid-conversation and eventually catch yourself in unfamiliar territory:

- *"What were we talking about again? How did we get here?"*
- *"Where did that thought come from? And where was it going?"*

That subtle reflection exposes a hidden motive indirectly guiding the conversation — a complementary element of substantive dialectic, and a vehicle for enriching a dialogue in unpredictable ways.

### User story

You read a captivating piece of writing composed of fragmented narratives, symbols, and closures. Interpreting the combination of these literary devices leaves you feeling a unique, profound emotion — one you could not summarize in a single word.

- *"Would I have reached this epiphany on my own?"*
- *"Could I have realized this insight in an alternative context?"*

Mindmap gives that reflection an object. Your sources become a graph you can hold, rearrange, question, and extend — so that tangents, latent themes, and unspoken motives become something you can actually see and follow.

### Background

- **Etymology** is the study of the historical development and evolution of words.
- **Epistemology** is the branch of philosophy that studies the nature, scope, and justification of human knowledge.
- Both terms derive from the Greek *episteme*, which Michel Foucault uses to name the underlying structure of assumptions that makes a given truth-claim utterable in the first place — the condition of possibility for truth and discourse in a given era.
- **Network science** is the study of complex network representations, identifying holistic properties that arise only through the interaction of their parts.

Mindmap sits at the intersection of these: it treats a body of language as a network, and reads that network back as discourse.
